3 Ways to Crowdsource Security: Open, Vetted and Private Bug Bounty Programs

Sep 2nd, 2014

Faced with a recent rise in online security threats and a shortage of talented security engineers, many businesses have turned to crowdsourced security to protect and defend their web applications. To demonstrate to the public that security is a top priority, businesses across the world have launched bug bounty programs to connect and engage directly with the security community. Due to the rise in popularity of these programs, there is an active, ongoing dialog within the information security community on topics surrounding vulnerability disclosure and response timelines in bug bounty programs. Because the security needs and resources of every business are different, there is no one-size-fits-all solution for vulnerability reporting, coordinating disclosure, and program administration for businesses and security researchers. When launching a bug bounty program, it is important to design your vulnerability management process and program structure while taking your company’s culture and resources into account. With that in mind, we wanted to take the time to explore some of the most common types of vulnerability disclosure programs for web application security: open, vetted and invitation only bug bounty programs.** Open Bug Bounty Programs** A fully open program is a public, open invitation program that allows anyone (and everyone) to disclose any potential security issues they come across in a website. These programs are typically run by large, consumer-facing technology companies like Facebook, Google and Paypal who each set their own disclosure policies within the scope of their respective programs.

Pros By running a program where anyone who finds a vulnerability can report a security vulnerability, these large companies with hundreds of millions of users can leverage the talents of anyone searching their site for security bugs. This means that talented security researchers, web application security enthusiasts, black hat hackers and even 10-year-old kids can submit a critical issue and help a business improve their security.

Cons Bug bounty hunting has become overwhelmingly popular over the last few years– and automatic scanning tools have allowed many researchers who do not have the sufficient skills to create a proof-of-concept to submit bug reports to these programs in hopes of receiving swag, a monetary reward, or a Hall of Fame mention. For program administrators, this results in a high volume of low quality reports that are duplicates, false positives, or issues that are out of the scope of the program.

This noise created by these low quality reports makes it difficult to distinguish irrelevant reports from critical issues, and this makes coordinating disclosure with researchers an extremely difficult task. After hitting delay after delay in the reporting and disclosure process, impatient researchers may choose to draw attention to the issues they’ve disclosed by publicly sharing them on a blog and across social media, forcing companies to scramble to correct these issues immediately. Compromises Because these open programs have set an example for responding to and rewarding researchers who have found valid vulnerabilities, some researchers expect all technology companies to run programs in this manner. Open programs require companies to make significant investments in resources for security and vulnerability management; the cost and resources required make it nearly impossible for small to medium sized businesses to implement this program model.

Vetted Bug Bounty Programs

For businesses who do not deal directly with millions of consumers, vetted bug bounty programs are an attractive alternative to an open, public program. These bug bounty programs are open only to vetted, verified security researchers who undergo a screening process before evaluating a site’s security or submitting vulnerability reports. Typically, these programs are hosted or managed through a third party bug bounty platform or security marketplace like CrowdCurity.

Pros By limiting program participation to a vetted group of security researchers, companies receive higher quality vulnerability reports with detailed, better articulated steps to reproduce vulnerabilities from program participants. Though these researchers may still use automated tools to assist their security research, they are qualified to search manually for vulnerabilities that require more specialized skills and time to discover. By working with talented researchers, the noise created by low quality vulnerability reports is eliminated, making it easier for businesses to collaborate directly with researchers and focus on fixing critical security issues.

Cons While vetted bug bounty programs solve the problem of removing the noise and distraction of low-quality vulnerability reports, they make it difficult for researchers protective of their anonymity or who have not yet built a track record of successful security research to submit vulnerabilities. Researchers who do not undergo vetting or who are rejected from participating in these programs must rely on alternative channels to to report security issues. Compromises By running bug bounty programs with vetted security researchers, startups, emerging technology companies and smaller businesses can open a channel with the security community that requires fewer resources than a fully open program. A vetted bug bounty program limits the noise of a more open, public program by leveraging the talents of established researchers known for high quality security work, though businesses will still need to monitor their communications channels for vulnerability disclosures from researchers unable to participate in these programs.

Private Bug Bounty Programs

Out of all of the bug bounty programs, invitation-only bug bounty programs are the most exclusive in security research. Details about the scope and the vulnerabilities uncovered during the duration of these programs are not readily available to the wider public, though companies may choose to coordinate disclosure with researchers on a case-by-case basis. Invitation-only bug bounty programs are highly exclusive— they engage and reward the the best, most talented security researchers for using their specialized skills in finding security vulnerabilities.

Pros These closed programs are designed for a small, elite group of testers who are selectively chosen to search for security issues. Security researchers are required to demonstrate certain skills to attract the attention of the company hosting the program, which results in high quality vulnerability research and reporting focused on patching highly critical issues. These programs also allow companies to run security tests on features that are not in full, public release, especially for closed-source technologies that provide critical services for their users.
